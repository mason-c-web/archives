Keith Haring Exhibit

The MoPop was hosting a Keith Haring Exhibit and I was set on going and finally got a chance. Even if you are unfamiliar, you have probably seen his work. Keith Haring was a pop artist and AIDS activist in the New York art scene in the 80s. His career was a short one, basically spanning from 1982-1890.

His work is colorful and fun, and often uses the same iconography. The barking dog, the radiant baby, people dancing or hugging. I found him a fascinating mix of politically active, and commercial (though still anti capitalist). For a time, he had a store in NY where you could buy shirts, pins, and an inflatable baby.

His work is really remembered in context of the US AIDS Epidemic. What I know about the AIDS epidemic in the 80's in New York is mostly what I learned from the 1994 musical, RENT, so researching more was interesting. Nowadays, HIV/AIDs is manageable with medication, but in the 1980's it was basically a death sentence. It disproportionately affected the gay community, and the stigma around it was unjustly terrible. The US Government was dangerously slow to action, most definitely because it was seen as a "gay disease". Ronald Regan didn't publicly mention AIDs until 1985. Keith was a politically active openly gay man during this time period, so a lot of his work talked about these injustices happening. Tragically, he was diagnosed with AIDs in 1988, and died in 1990, ending his career.

"Unfinished Painting" was not at this exhibit, but I think it is one of my favorite of his works. It represents the unfinished work and lives of those who died from AIDs.

To be clear, the thing that brings me joy here is seeing art, learning and researching history, and thinking about the life and times of someone who made a meaningful impact on the world. The context in which something was created is the meaningful part of the art. To explore and understand that helps me inform my own work.

For a while now I've been reading through the 3rd Body Problem Trilogy by Liu Cixin. It is a highly rated sci-fi series so I wanted to try it out. They are translated from their original Chinese, and some percentage of the book is lost on me because of that. There are lots of references to Chinese philosophers and a couple of puns that had to be explained in the footnotes.

The main plot is about what happens to humanity after first contact with aliens. These books are fiction, but are so scientific at points they are borderline educational. There is a whole bit where they build a computer out of men with black and white flags while fully explaining how CPUs work.  

The best character is Shi Qiang (or Da Shi if you are friends, I think it's the equivalent of "Big Man Shi"). A grizzled private detective who chain smokes, and somehow the smartest character in a book full of physicists. He encourages the main character not to give up in the face of peril. He's ready to hit the town for a night of drinking at the drop of a hat. He saves a man's life from a couch that is trying to eat him. Everyone loves him cause he is the best.

Every other character in this book is completely forgettable. That's because this book is not really about the characters at any point. Most people come and go quickly out of the story as it spans literal centuries. But in the end, it's about SCIENCE and PHILOSOPHY and POLITICS. I do respect it for this, but also I got bored. I read the first two and half of the 3rd one before I gave up and read the summary online. I found them personally to be a bit of a slog. They are also not hopeful in any way; the ending is extremely bleak. However, some of the ideas in this book are so interesting and fun. What would society look like if we had cryo sleep? What do we do in the face of certain peril, and why is it have a town orgy? Why do we keep using Australia as a prison?  How can I get a couch that forms perfectly to my body (and will it try to eat me)? I would get to a fun section and eat it up, only to be bored again in 20 pages. 

I don't know if I can super recommend these books. If you love sci fi and are interested, don't read anything about them, and try the first book. I think it is very much a love it or hate it kind of thing.

However, there are 3 TV show adaptations:
  • A 2023 Chinese show which I have heard that stays pretty accurate to books, and is popular in China. You can watch it on Amazon Prime. (The culture impact of these books in China vs US is an interesting topic for another day.)
  • The 2024 Netflix show; It doesn't capture the tone or spirit of the book at all IMO. Also they set it in London and changed the race of most of the characters to not Chinese. However, I feel the most interesting storylines are there, and this is kind of the most consumable version.
  • However, the best adaptation, as praised by fans online? The MineCraft fan series.
